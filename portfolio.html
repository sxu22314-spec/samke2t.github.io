Between Us: Motivation & Research

1. Motivation

Why this topic

We selected the intergenerational theme because family relationships are emotionally important but often under-supported by digital systems. Many apps focus on speed, entertainment, or productivity, while meaningful family communication can be slow, uneven, and difficult to start. We were interested in designing a playful experience that supports small moments of connection between children, parents, and grandparents.

Our design direction focuses on reducing awkwardness. Instead of asking users to “talk more” in a vague way, we prototype concrete interaction tools: prompts, triggers, shared responses, and digital capsules. The goal is not to solve every family problem, but to create a more inviting first step.

2. Research snapshot

Early desk research and market scan

Click title to enter source page.

Relational Design for Remote Intergenerational Sensorial Play (2020)

  • Did well: Emphasized non-verbal bonding through tactile feedback.
  • Did well: Reduced "performance anxiety" in conversation.
  • Did well: Created a "third space" beyond simple video screens.
  • Missed: Required specialized hardware (sensors) not found in homes.
  • Missed: High latency issues for real-time synchronization.
  • Missed: Short-term engagement; lacked a narrative "hook."

Story-Me: Intergenerational Storytelling and Preservation (2020)

  • Did well: Strong focus on long-term heritage and legacy.
  • Did well: Scaffolded prompts to help seniors recall memories.
  • Did well: Validated the emotional value of "being heard."
  • Missed: Passive for youth: Grandkids were often just "listeners."
  • Missed: High cognitive load for uploading/organizing media.
  • Missed: Lack of "Playfulness" (too much like an interview).

Tangible VR-Based System for Intergenerational Learning (2022)

  • Did well: Combined physical objects with virtual environments.
  • Did well: Enhanced spatial presence (feeling "together").
  • Did well: Used gamification to drive the learning process.
  • Missed: VR Sickness: Physical discomfort for many elderly users.
  • Missed: Isolation: High-end headsets hide facial expressions.
  • Missed: Unnatural interaction: Controllers were too complex.

Empirical Study of VR to Promote Intergenerational Communication (2021)

  • Did well: Provided quantitative data on user satisfaction.
  • Did well: Identified key "ice-breaking" mechanics in VR.
  • Did well: Focused on mutual empathy between age groups.
  • Missed: Excluded seniors with moderate visual impairments.
  • Missed: The setup was too bulky for a typical living room.
  • Missed: Over-relied on technical support during the study.

FaceTime / Zoom (Video Conferencing)

  • Did well: Extreme stability across various network conditions.
  • Did well: High market reach and universal user familiarity.
  • Did well: Simple, frictionless UI for establishing basic calls.
  • Missed: Passive interaction (limited to verbal/visual).
  • Missed: "Video call fatigue" during extended sessions.
  • Missed: Lack of integrated shared playful activities.

Roblox (Social Play)

  • Did well: Infinite creativity and user-generated content.
  • Did well: Highly playful mechanics and avatar-based expression.
  • Did well: Instant real-time social interaction in 3D spaces.
  • Missed: Steep learning curve for non-tech-savvy seniors.
  • Missed: Complex 3D navigation and multi-button controls.
  • Missed: Chaotic social environment unsuitable for quiet bonding.

StorySign (AI-Powered Sign Language Tool)

  • Did well: Innovative use of AI avatars for real-time translation.
  • Did well: Addresses a highly specific social niche (deaf literacy).
  • Did well: High emotional impact on parent-child bonding.
  • Missed: One-way information flow; lacks reciprocal play.
  • Missed: Highly dependent on specific physical book titles.
  • Missed: Lacks intergenerational co-creation features.

Osmo / AR Storybooks (Phygital Systems)

  • Did well: Excellent tangible feedback using physical objects.
  • Did well: Encourages "heads-up" play instead of screen staring.
  • Did well: Highly intuitive interaction for younger children.
  • Missed: Primarily designed for co-located (in-person) play.
  • Missed: High hardware dependency (requires base/reflector).
  • Missed: Thematic content often feels too "childish" for seniors.

3. Stakeholders and personas

Primary and secondary users

Primary user

Mei, 10

A child who enjoys playful mobile interactions but sometimes finds conversations with grandparents slow or difficult to continue.

  • Goal: talk with family in a more fun way.
  • Pain point: does not know what to ask next.
  • Need: visual prompts and quick responses.

Primary user

Mr. Chen, 68

A grandparent who values family connection and memories, but may feel uncertain when using new digital tools or starting emotional topics.

  • Goal: stay close to grandchildren.
  • Pain point: technology can feel intimidating.
  • Need: simple interface and familiar triggers.

Secondary user

Lin, 39

A parent who wants to support communication between older and younger family members but has limited time and attention.

  • Goal: support family bonding.
  • Pain point: busy schedule and emotional labour.
  • Need: low-friction, repeatable sessions.

4. User journey

Current pain points before the design intervention

01

Before meeting

Family members want to connect but do not prepare topics or shared activities.

02

Start of interaction

Conversation begins with routine questions and quickly slows down.

03

Middle of interaction

One side speaks more, the other side struggles to respond or ask follow-up questions.

04

After interaction

No shared record remains, so positive moments are not easily revisited or built upon later.

5. Requirements

Three must-have playful qualities

  • Easy to start: the system should reduce the pressure of thinking of the “right” thing to say.
  • Easy to share: both generations should contribute to the interaction, not just consume content.
  • Easy to revisit: the outcome should create a trace that can be saved, replayed, or extended later.

6. Ideation and alternatives

Three concept directions considered

These alternatives are presented as draft design routes. The selected direction is the hybrid card + capsule model.

Alternative A: Story Map

A location-based memory map for family stories attached to places.

Strength: rich context.

Weakness: more setup and cognitive load.

Alternative B: Prompt + Capsule

Short questions, familiar triggers, emotional reactions, and saved mini-capsules.

Strength: lightweight, repeatable, mobile-friendly.

Weakness: needs careful content design.

Alternative C: Voice Challenge

Voice-based mini tasks for family members to respond asynchronously.

Strength: expressive and personal.

Weakness: harder to review quickly and less private in shared spaces.

8. Evaluation and reflection

Alpha testing, iteration, and ethical considerations

Usability testing plan

Test the alpha prototype with three users from mixed age groups and observe whether they can understand the flow without explanation.

Before / after iteration

Example change: reduce visual clutter, increase text contrast, and simplify the “save capsule” action into one clear CTA.

Social and ethical reflection

The design should not force emotional disclosure. It should allow users to skip prompts, control sharing, and avoid making family intimacy feel performative.

AI use reflection

This draft prototype uses AI-assisted front-end scaffolding. The final portfolio should document prompts, verification steps, and accessibility checks.
